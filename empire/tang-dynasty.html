Central & East Asia

Tang Dynasty

Peak740 CE
Duration289 yrs
Period618 CE – 907 CE

Foundation

The Tang Dynasty was founded in 618 CE by Li Yuan (Emperor Gaozu), a Sui dynasty military commander who exploited the catastrophic collapse of the Sui — brought down by the disastrous Korean campaigns of Emperor Yang and the massive peasant rebellions they provoked — to seize the imperial capital Chang'an and establish a new dynasty. The Tang's founding was a family enterprise of unusual sophistication: Li Yuan's second son, Li Shimin, played the decisive military role in the campaigns that secured the dynasty, and in 626 CE staged the Xuanwu Gate Incident — an ambush in which he killed his two brothers, including the crown prince Jiancheng, and compelled his father to abdicate in his favour. As Emperor Taizong (r. 626–649), Li Shimin presided over what Chinese tradition has celebrated as a golden age of humane governance, military power, and cultural creativity. His administration of the northern frontier was particularly impressive: rather than pursuing the harsh exclusionary policies of his Sui predecessors, Taizong incorporated Turkic and other non-Han peoples into the Tang military and administrative system, earning the unprecedented title 'Heavenly Khagan' from the steppe peoples — a recognition that the Tang empire straddled the Chinese and Inner Asian political worlds simultaneously. The brief interruption of the Tang succession by the Empress Wu Zetian (r. 690–705), who established her own Zhou dynasty, was followed by the Kaiyuan era under Emperor Xuanzong (r. 713–741), regarded by Chinese historians as the Tang's cultural and administrative zenith, during which the population reached approximately 53 million and Chang'an was the largest and most cosmopolitan city on earth.

Socioeconomy

The Tang economy was the most sophisticated and commercially integrated in the world during the dynasty's peak, sustained by an agricultural base transformed by the equal-field (juntian) land allocation system and connected to transcontinental trade networks through the overland Silk Road and the maritime routes of the South China Sea. Chang'an, with an estimated population of one million within the walls and significantly more in the surrounding metropolitan area, was the world's largest city and a commercial centre of extraordinary cosmopolitanism: Sogdian, Persian, Arab, Indian, Korean, Japanese, and Central Asian merchants maintained permanent quarters in the city's designated foreign merchant wards, exchanging silk, porcelain, and Chinese manufactured goods for horses, spices, aromatics, glassware, and luxury commodities from every corner of Eurasia. The equal-field system, which allocated agricultural land to adult male subjects in return for tax and military service obligations, provided a relatively stable agricultural base during the dynasty's early and middle periods, though it broke down progressively as noble and monastic land accumulation concentrated holdings and reduced the state's ability to allocate land to new households. The development of wood-block printing during the Tang period, used primarily for Buddhist texts and calendars, represented a technological innovation whose commercial implications would not be fully realised until the Song. The catastrophic An Lushan Rebellion (755–763), in which the Tang general An Lushan rebelled with his northeastern army, sacked Chang'an, and forced Emperor Xuanzong into flight, fundamentally disrupted the equal-field system, depopulated much of northern China, and forced the dynasty to rely on increasingly autonomous regional military governors (jiedushi) whose fiscal independence permanently weakened imperial revenues.

Military

The Tang military was the most formidable in the world during the early and middle dynasty periods, combining the disciplined infantry and cavalry traditions of the Chinese agricultural core with the steppe cavalry expertise of Turkic, Sogdian, and other Inner Asian commanders and soldiers incorporated into the imperial service. The府兵 (fubing) conscript system, inherited from the Sui and refined by the early Tang, provided a citizen-soldier force whose military obligations were linked to land allocation under the equal-field system, creating a self-financing military that did not burden the state with permanent professional army costs. The early Tang campaigns under Taizong were extraordinary in their scope and success: the destruction of the Eastern Turkic Khaganate in 630, the conquest of Gaochang (640), the defeat of the Western Turkic Khaganate, and campaigns that extended Tang influence to the Tarim Basin and the borders of Persia placed Chinese power at its greatest westward extent. The Battle of Talas River in 751, where a Tang force was defeated by an Abbasid-allied army, is often cited as a strategic limit-marker for Tang western expansion — and incidentally the occasion on which Chinese paper-making technology was transmitted westward through captured craftsmen. The An Lushan Rebellion (755–763) destroyed the fubing system by demonstrating that the northeastern frontier armies, whose professional character and regional autonomy distinguished them from the conscript fubing, were militarily more powerful than the conscript forces and politically uncontrollable. After 763, the dynasty was increasingly dependent on warlord military commanders whose loyalty could not be reliably coerced, and the military balance between the imperial centre and regional power-holders never recovered the equilibrium of the early Tang.

Governance & Law

The Tang governance system was the most sophisticated administrative framework in the medieval world, built on the three departments and six ministries (sansheng liubu) structure that became the template for Chinese imperial administration for the subsequent millennium. The three departments — the Secretariat, the Chancellery, and the Department of State Affairs — provided overlapping institutional checks on policy-making, requiring imperial edicts to pass through successive stages of drafting, review, and implementation that created genuine institutional constraints on arbitrary royal decision-making. The six ministries (Personnel, Revenue, Rites, War, Justice, and Works) administered the functional domains of government through hierarchically organised bureaucracies staffed by officials selected through the civil service examination system. Emperor Taizong's relationship with his ministers, particularly the remonstrance official Wei Zheng whose blunt criticisms the emperor famously accepted, became a model of sagely governance that Chinese political thought idealised for centuries. The legal code — the Tang Code (Tang Lü Shuyi), promulgated in 653 — was a comprehensive legal compilation that influenced the legal systems of Japan, Korea, and Vietnam, demonstrating the reach of Tang institutional influence across East Asia. The collapse of central authority after the An Lushan Rebellion forced the dynasty to tolerate the institutionalisation of the jiedushi system, in which regional military governors controlled taxation, appointments, and military forces in their territories, creating a quasi-feudal decentralisation that the late Tang was never able to reverse and that ultimately produced the Five Dynasties period that followed the dynasty's fall.

Global & Regional Impact

The Tang Dynasty's global historical impact operated through multiple channels simultaneously: the commercial integration of the Eurasian continent through the Silk Road, the cultural and institutional influence of Tang China on its East Asian neighbours, and the technological innovations — printing, porcelain, and later gunpowder — that would eventually transform world history. Chang'an's cosmopolitanism during the early Tang period was genuinely without equal in the world: Nestorian Christian, Zoroastrian, Manichaean, Islamic, and Buddhist religious communities all maintained temples and monasteries in the capital, while the government employed Sogdian financial experts, Turkic military commanders, Korean administrators, and Japanese cultural observers. The Tang tributary system integrated Japan, Korea, and Vietnam into a hierarchical diplomatic framework organised around periodic tribute missions to Chang'an, transmitting Chinese administrative models, writing systems, Buddhist institutions, and material culture to these societies with consequences that persisted for centuries beyond the Tang's fall. Japanese administrative reforms of the Taika (645) and Taiho (701) eras explicitly modelled themselves on Tang institutional precedents. Korean kingdoms absorbed Tang administrative law, examination systems, and Buddhist culture. Vietnam's prolonged period of direct Tang administration (679–939) left deep institutional and cultural marks on Vietnamese society. The porcelain technology developed to its first classical perfection under Tang patronage — the gleaming white Xing ware and the three-colour (sancai) glazed wares — reached markets across the Islamic world and East Africa, establishing Chinese ceramics as a prestige commodity in world trade.

Decline & Fall

The Tang Dynasty's decline followed a long arc of structural deterioration punctuated by the An Lushan Rebellion's immediate catastrophe and culminating in the dynasty's final dissolution under the warlordism of the late ninth century. The An Lushan Rebellion (755–763) was the pivot point: the death of approximately one-third of China's registered population, the destruction of the equal-field agricultural system, the rise of the militarily autonomous jiedushi, and the permanent weakening of central fiscal authority created conditions from which the dynasty never fully recovered.

The Huang Chao Rebellion (874–884) — a massive peasant uprising that sacked both Chang'an and Luoyang — was the terminal blow: it destroyed what remained of the institutional fabric of Tang central governance, killed enormous numbers of the educated elite who staffed the bureaucracy, and transferred effective power to warlords who used the crisis to consolidate regional dominance. The last Tang emperors were puppets of competing warlords, and the dynasty's formal end came in 907 when Zhu Wen, a former rebel general who had served the dynasty before betraying it, forced the last emperor to abdicate and founded the Later Liang. The subsequent Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms period (907–960) was a prolonged political fragmentation, but it preserved much of the Tang institutional and cultural inheritance and produced the commercial and agricultural innovations that the Song Dynasty would systematise. The Tang's legacy — its administrative templates, legal codes, literary culture, Buddhist institutions, and commercial networks — was so thoroughly embedded in East Asian civilisation that subsequent Chinese dynasties defined themselves as inheritors of the Tang model, and its influence on Japan, Korea, and Vietnam persisted long after the dynasty itself had fallen. The Tang Dynasty represents the apex of Chinese imperial cosmopolitanism — the moment when Chang'an was genuinely the centre of the world, drawing talent, commerce, and culture from every corner of Eurasia and radiating Chinese institutional and cultural models across East and Southeast Asia with consequences that persist to the present day.
