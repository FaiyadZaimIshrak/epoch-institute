Central & East Asia

Gokturk Empire

Peak710 CE
Duration198 yrs
Period546 CE – 744 CE

Foundation

The Göktürk Empire was born from an insult. Bumin, leader of the Ashina clan and a vassal of the Rouran Khaganate, crushed a Uyghur revolt in 546 CE and expected a Rouran princess as reward. The khagan refused, calling him a blacksmith slave. Bumin allied with Western Wei China, married a Wei princess, and in 552 CE destroyed the Rouran in a decisive battle, declaring himself Qaghan and becoming the first leader to use Türk as a political name. He died within months. Under Muqan Qaghan (553–572), expansion was extraordinary: the remaining Rouran were exterminated, the Khitans defeated eastward, and Muqan's brother Istämi drove westward past the Caspian into former Hephthalite territory. By 568 CE, the Göktürks sent their first embassy to Constantinople, proposing alliance against the Sasanian Empire. The Eastern Khaganate extracted 100,000 rolls of silk annually from the Northern Zhou dynasty. By 576 CE, Göktürk forces had crossed into Crimea, the furthest westward reach of any Turkic power before the Mongols. The combined First Khaganate at its peak was likely the largest contiguous territorial entity on earth.

Socioeconomy

The Göktürk economy rested on tribute extraction and Silk Road management. Sogdian merchant partners handled long-distance commercial logistics under Göktürk military protection, making the khaganate indispensable to Silk Road commerce without requiring nomadic Göktürks to administer markets directly. Tribute from China was the single most valuable income stream. The Eastern Khaganate extracted silk, grain, and manufactured goods from successive Chinese dynasties in a relationship the Chinese framed as gifts but which functioned as tribute paid to avoid military devastation. Under Bilge Qaghan, a single diplomatic exchange with Tang Emperor Xuanzong produced 100,000 bolts of silk in return for thirty horses, a ratio that illustrates the extraction mechanism precisely. Pastoralism remained the economic base of the tribal population, with horse breeding and seasonal migration sustaining the military manpower that made all other income streams possible.

Military

Göktürk military power rested on mobile cavalry capable of rapid strategic concentration across vast steppe distances. The First Khaganate simultaneously extracted tribute from the Northern Zhou, Qi, and Sui dynasties, demonstrating credible military threat on multiple fronts. Qapaghan Qaghan (691–716), the peak military commander of the Second Khaganate, crossed the Yellow River six times between 693 and 710 CE, forcing Tang tribute payments. A Tang army of 80,000 was defeated at the Battle of Ming Sha by a significantly smaller Göktürk force. Qapaghan conquered the Yenisei Kyrgyz and killed their qaghan in personal combat in 710, extending influence from Manchuria to the Syr Darya. The empire's central military vulnerability was institutional: lateral succession, passing power among brothers before sons, guaranteed a crisis at every transition. The Sui dynasty's Chancellor Su Wei documented the Chinese strategy explicitly: set the barbarians against each other, never let them combine.

Governance & Law

The Göktürk state was a tribal confederation organised around the supremacy of the Ashina clan, whose divine mandate was expressed through the concept of heaven-granted qut, or royal fortune. The qaghan commanded subordinate tribal leaders who owed military service and tribute in exchange for protection and a share of extracted wealth. The Second Khaganate produced the Orkhon Inscriptions, erected between 732 and 735 CE, the earliest substantial written records in any Turkic language. These monuments are simultaneously a governance document, a legitimacy claim, and a warning against Chinese cultural assimilation, condemning Turkic nobles who had adopted Tang titles as traitors. The inscriptions reveal a regime acutely aware of its own fragility, using permanent stone monuments to enforce political loyalty that military power alone could not guarantee. Administration beyond the Mongolian heartland was exercised through subordinate tribal leaders rather than appointed governors, a structural limitation that made the empire's cohesion entirely dependent on the personal authority of the reigning qaghan.

Global & Regional Impact

The Göktürk Empire's significance extends far beyond its 192-year lifespan. The First Khaganate established the Silk Road's greatest period of security, enabling movement of goods, religions, and ideas between China, Persia, Byzantium, and India under a single protective military umbrella. The Orkhon script, the first written Turkic language, created a literary tradition that influenced subsequent steppe civilisations. The Ashina clan's confederation model was inherited by the Uyghur, Khazar, and Bulgar khaganates, and the broader Turkic political tradition eventually flowed into the Seljuk and Ottoman empires. The 568 CE embassy to Constantinople established a direct diplomatic relationship between the steppe world and Byzantium that shaped Byzantine foreign policy for generations. The Orkhon Inscriptions remain the foundational texts of Turkic literary heritage, studied today as the earliest window into Turkic political thought.

Decline & Fall

The First Khaganate collapsed in 603 CE through the mechanism its enemies had cultivated: lateral succession produced simultaneous rival claimants, the Sui exploited the divisions, and the unified empire fragmented. The Eastern Khaganate was conquered by Tang China in 630 CE, its qaghan captured in a surprise night attack by the general Li Jing. The Western Khaganate fell in 657 CE. Between 679 and 681, two revolts failed and fifty-four Göktürk nobles were publicly executed in the eastern market of Chang'an. The resurrection came in 682 CE when Ilterish Qaghan began with seventeen followers in the Yin Shan mountains and rebuilt the Second Khaganate through guerrilla warfare, peaking under Qapaghan and consolidated under Bilge. The deaths of Tonyukuk in 726, Kül Tigin in 731, and Bilge himself in 734, poisoned by his own chancellor, removed the entire governing cohort within eight years. In 744 CE, the Uyghurs, Karluks, and Basmyls seized Ötüken, beheaded the last Göktürk qaghan, and sent his head to the Tang court.

The Göktürk Empire's enduring significance lies in its double destruction and double resurrection: a nomadic people with no cities, no agriculture, and no written language built the largest empire on earth, lost it entirely to foreign conquest, rebuilt it from seventeen men, and left a civilisational legacy that shaped every major Turkic state for the next thousand years.
